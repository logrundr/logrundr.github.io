On the State of Modern "Classical Music"

Article 001, 2022/7/27


What is Music? Why does it affect us so deeply?

Music is, at its core, entirely rhythm. To the Universe, there exists no concept of pitch, only rhythm. And every interval is merely a polyrhythm—that is, two rhythms played simultaneously. Every interval in music has a ratio between the frequencies of the pitches played. Frequency is the amount of times per second that waves are sent through air, called Hertz or Hz. A ratio of 2:3 means that for every 2 waves sent by the first frequency, 3 are sent by the second. This can be thought of as a 2:3 polyrhythm. When played sufficiently fast, it sounds a perfect 5th (see 1 for a demonstration). In this way, music is entirely rhythm.

The ratios between intervals is what ultimately affects us and gives music its emotion. The octave, a ratio of 2:1, is the simplest interval in music (besides the unison of 1:1), and therefore doesn't affect us very much. On the other hand, there are ratios which are too complex to be emotional, like a ratio of 1957:1743. The key is finding ratios in between which aren't too simple, neither are they too complex.

There is also of course the many other aspects of music besides pure pitch. There is tempo, rhythm, dynamics, and articulations. Music is incredibly complex, and each of these aspects brings further emotion out of the player and into their instrument (including the voice).

What is Classical Music?

The term classical music is actually quite broad; however, it generally refers to the longstanding formal tradition of music, especially in the Western world. It also typically has complex musical forms, harmonic structures, polyphony, and is almost always written down (2).

The formality and complexity of classical music is what separates it from the folk tradition. This article will only be focusing on the Western tradition of classical music, although some aspects can be applied to other traditions as well.

Music in the Era of Recording

The advent of the phonograph changed the music scene forever. Suddenly you didn't have to write music to get your music known, you simply had to be able to play and record it. This allowed many folk and amateur musicians to record their work and spread it around. The classical composer Bela Bartók sought to preserve Hungarian folk songs by recording folk singers on an Edison phonograph (3).

Suddenly all types of music were equally accessible, and an orchestra and opera singers would be just as loud as a folk musician with a guitar. As it turns out, the guitar was the primary instrument of blues and folk musicians in the early 20th century, and the steady chugging rhythms of the Delta Blues style was incredibly influential upon rock musicians.

The way in which music was viewed changed. Before the phonograph, music was a rare thing typically played only in social settings like Salons or at music concerts like Operas or Recitals. After the phonograph, music could be played at any time and for any reason, including outside of social settings. People began to play music in the background while doing other things. This is a very common usage of today's popular music. Many people put on music to play in the background while studying, doing homework, washing the dishes, or driving their car. Music has become simply noise to stimulate our ears while we do mundane tasks.

Classical music requires intent listening—it needs attention in order for the listener to grasp all of the thematic changes and development. Modern music is generally quite repetitive and therefore works better as a piece of background music due to a lack of development. An extreme example of repetitiveness and "backgroundness" is what is today called "elevator music" or "muzak", but at the time of its conception was called "furniture music".

The Advent of "Furniture Music"

The classical composer Erik Satie envisioned a kind of music never before created. In his time, music was art, and art demanded that attention be paid to it. He created the idea of background music, which he called "furniture music". In 1917 Satie commented this to a friend:

Furniture music is something that should be created—that is, music would be part of the ambience, which would take account of it. I imagine it being melodic in nature: it would soften the noise of knives and forks without dominating them, without imposing itself. It would furnish those silences which sometimes hang heavy between diners. It would save them from everyday banalities. At the same time, it would neutralise those street sounds which impinge on us indiscreetly. (4)

Furniture music was a crucial step in removing the art from music and turning it just into a piece of furniture—something to be ignored and blend into the environment in the same way that a chair or a rug does. Furniture music was made to be boring and repetitive because no one was meant to pay attention to it (5, and see 6).

The idea of music meant to blend into the background was expanded when jazz became popular. Jazz in its most popular form was subtle, smooth, mellow, and quietly melodic; in other words, it was the perfect sound for blending into the background. Of course, many other genres of modern music are played in the background, but jazz perhaps works the best out of all of them, especially since many jazz tunes are only instrumental, allowing people to work on whatever they're doing without being distracted by lyrics.

The problem is that the creation of background music destroyed not only its own art, but also the art of all other music. Because background music, and other genres which could act as background music (which is essentially all of modern music), had become so popular, perception of music in general had shifted into a more casual mindset, classical music included. Modern performers of classical music began, subconsciously or not, to incorporate the values of modern music into classical, attempting to destroy the art in classical and replace it with the ability to be played in the background.

On the Tasteful Playing of Classical Music

There is a general opinion that I have seen which states that the great pianists of the 19th and early 20th century played music, especially the piano, tastelessly and with too much rubato or embellishments. Whereas, I believe that more modern classical music recordings are allowing the ideas of modern genres like rock or pop to permeate into the interpretations of the much older classical music.

Modern genres, particularly rock, are based on groove—the music is primarily meant to be something that you can stamp your feet, bob your head, or tap your fingers to. Because of this, rubato in modern genres is either nonexistent (due to being recorded to a click track) or very subtle. This idea of eliminating rubato has managed to make its way into classical recordings despite most classical music not relying on a groove like modern music does. Because of this recent approach, opinions towards older classical performances and descriptions of how classical music used to be performed have been more negative in recent years.

The pianists of the 19th and early 20th century were in fact much more expressive in their use of rubato, but also in all aspects of their playing. Improvisation within classical performances is extremely rare and even discouraged, because modern society does not value personal emotional expression as strongly as it once did.

We are living today through an age of professionalism, which stifles creative expression and instead prioritises the exact recreation of the sheet music.

Modern Professionalism

Society's aesthetic decisions have led to professionalism: long gone are the days of elaborate and intricate woodwork as found in Baroque and Romantic architecture and furniture, as well as the incredibly busy interior decoration of the Victorian Era (see 7, 8). The 1960s saw a boom in modernism and minimalism, in areas from architecture to furniture to graphic design, and yes, to music.

The Romantics of the 1800s emphasised emotion, the expression of the individual, idealisation of nature, and suspicion of science and industrialisation (9). By the 1950s, a counterculture had emerged in youth from rock'n'roll, and by the 1960s, that counterculture grew immensely and became mainstream and political, focusing on love, sexuality, and, perhaps most famously, anti-war and anti-government sentiments. In many ways the so-called counterculture of the 1960s followed the tenets of Romanticism: hippies are well known for expression of emotion and the self, especially sexually; of their idealisation of nature and the natural self; and their inclination to reject science for more "herbal" approaches. This "counterculture" was actually an extreme embodiment of Romanticism, creating psychedelic rock in the tradition of popular music, and the real counterculture was the culture promoting minimalism, brutalism, modernism, and ugly, hard to relate to works. This was a direct counter to the at times overwhelming and overbearing intricacy, complexity, and beauty of the Victorian, Edwardian, and Art Deco Eras that the earlier generations had lived through.

This culture of ugliness didn't crop up out of nowhere. It was born from a generation who had grown up in the midst or wake of the Great Depression, and who had lived through World War II. It's no wonder that ugliness and minimalism were their primary modes of expression. In terms of music, the dispensing of many notes and focus on simplicity is what living in the Great Depression was forced to be, with limited money, limited food, and a need to focus on the small things to remain sane.

The government was ultimately partly responsible in allowing the Depression to get as severe as it did, but they were also responsible for getting the country out of it—people looked to the country for assistance desperately. Even long after the Depression had ended, and quality of life was actually at an all-time high, people remained stuck in their ways, perhaps of frugality, most importantly of reliance on authority.

The reliance on authority extends to academic spheres. And in academic spheres, it is largely unacceptable to show emotion in professional works. The burgeoning counterculture of minimalists, brutalists, and "modern art" infiltrated academic spheres—these same people were the ones being criticised by academics decades earlier within the 1920s and '30s as "degenerates". The formal art of classical music, once treated in the past as an extension of values regarding self expression and emotion, was now transformed into a sterilised academic form of study and research. Classical music was allowed to become this because it was old: the new tradition of pop music had surpassed it in popularity years before. Sheet music was no longer considered a means for creating emotional art; instead it showed the performer exact instructions, like a scientist's procedure for an experiment, or, perhaps, like a computer following an algorithm.

Analog by Computer

A digital computer operates on 1s and 0s. An analog computer is one which uses an analog to simulate and solve a real world problem. The earliest example of a complex analog computer is the Antikythera mechanism (Μηχανισμός Αντικυθήρων), discovered in 1901 in a shipwreck off the coast of the Greek island of Antikythera. The machine dates to around 150 BCE, and contains over 30 gears used for predicting astronomical positions and eclipses (10).

Analog computers such as the Antikythera mechanism aren't exact: the turning of the gears is what tells you the answer, not some computation of 1s and 0s, and because of that, reproducing the same answer twice is impossible—the mechanism has an infinite range of possible inputs. Digital computers by comparison are much more robust, but some amount of variability and intuitiveness is lost there. Digital computers are logical and versatile, but the simple attractiveness of a set of gears capable of predicting complex astronomical problems is compelling.

It's the same way in music. There is no space in music for a binary—a simple on or off. Music must have a range of dynamics, articulations, and tempo. And a performance which has such a range is not just compelling and attractive, but also capable of conveying extremely rich emotions. A binary performance is one without soul, the same as a computer does not have sentience. AI uses algorithms to get what it's after, and the results are completely without awareness. An AI generating an image of a flower field doesn't really know what a flower is, or the sky, or any of the individual components of such an image that a human might be interested in. It simply compiles images of flower fields and generates a new one based on those.

Humans are more original than AI, though. Humans don't just copy what others do, they are capable of originality, and of looking at the little things to paint a more accurate and compelling big picture. A person who has understanding of what a flower looks like, or what a blade of grass is, will be able to paint a more realistic scene of a flower field. Similarly, someone who is able to take a piece of music and interpret it, pull it apart, dissect its emotions, and also have the willingness to play with such a range too, will play better than one who only pays attention to what the sheet music says and who follows it exactly to the letter. As the great Franz Liszt puts it:

Music embodies feeling without forcing it to contend and combine with thought, as it is forced in most arts and especially in the art of words. ... Music presents at once the intensity and the expression of feeling. (11)

Music is meant to be spontaneous, never reproducible, and always played according to how the player feels while they are playing, and not according to how they prepared themselves to feel. In the sense of music's irreproducibility, it is a bit like an analog computer, which, because of the infinite range of possible inputs, it is impossible to precisely match the same input twice and therefore impossible to receive precisely the same output. Thus is the way of the human mind.
