Glossary


Activity vs. Task

an activity, being defined as receptive or creative: (1) the receptive activity: one where ideas are being received, (2) the creative activity: one where ideas are being conveyed.

a task, being defined as neither receptive or creative: a meaningless job; busywork.

The Art of Conversation

see Entry 551 "The Art of Conversation"

a type of active communication, in which both parties are actively participating in the exchange of ideas, and which contains two halves that each side takes turns sharing—that of speaking and listening.

Beautiful Person

see Entry 568 "Beautiful People, and Soulmates"

a person who has beauty on the inside (in their mind). This means someone who is insightful, philosophical, optimistic, creative, and ever-changing, in all the right ways—as well as being charming and all of those other charismatic traits. Someone who is never angry, and always has something beautiful to say about any situation. It is an ideal which can never truly be reached fully, only partially.
related: Philosophy of Beautiful Minds

Circular Narrative

a narrative which can begin at any point and circle back to wherever it began while maintaining a cohesive and satisfying plot and allowing the story to loop infinitely.

Communication

see Entry 551 "The Art of Conversation"

the exchange of ideas

  • two ideas
    • the idea suggested by the semantics: literal meaning of what is conveyed
    • the idea suggested by the diction: the way in which it is said
  • two roles
    • speaker/sender: the active role
    • listener/receiver: the passive role
  • receptive vs. active communication
    • receptive communication: one-sided
    • active communication: sender and receiver roles are exchanged between participants

Deliberate Mutism

see Entry 523 "Deliberate Mutism"

a condition whereby the afflicted chooses not to speak in certain scenarios and actively avoids conversation, but without a strong associated anxiety. Distinguished from selective mutism, an actual psychological term.

Forced Love vs. Natural Love

see Entry 556 "Forced Love and Natural Love"

forced love: "settling" for someone lesser than what you know you really want; when you force yourself to try to love someone and you force yourself into a relationship despite a lack of real connection.

natural love: "soul-mates", in a way; the kind of love where you know right away that you love them, because everything about them is immediately perfect (in your perception).

Outlined Book

story with minimal details and descriptions of scenes, with just enough plot information. Most minutiae is left up to the reader to imagine (like exact dialogue and other small details). The setting is described well to place the reader in the story.

The Philosophy of Beautiful Minds

see Entry 562 "The Philosophy of Beautiful Minds"

a type of mindset which helps one to appreciate art more: whenever one is engaging with a work of art, either visual, spatial, aural, or some other medium, look past the art and imagine its creator—but further than just imagining the creator, imagine the creator's mind, and specifically ask yourself, "what kind of mind must theirs be in order to create such beautiful art?"

Pretender

see Entry 483 "Pretenders"

people who fake who they are to society, making themself appear better and more productive/helpful than they really are; and who waste their time doing nothing but say otherwise.

Sad Hopelessness vs. Sad Optimism

see Entry 574 "Sad Hopelessness and Sad Optimism"

sad hopelessness: depression, and feeling hopeless about your future.
sad optimism: depression, yet feeling optimistic about your future.

Utopia

an impossible world where everything is always perfect, forever, which goes against the most fundamental property of the Universe: that which says that everything is impermanent. Such a perfect world wouldn't have work, money, government (since there is no need to govern perfect individuals), death, pain, boredom, etc. It would be a world of Beautiful people.

Web of Discrimination

see Article 2 "Should Animals be Used for Food?"

all forms of discrimination are interconnected in a big web, informing and encouraging each other, and supporting each other to continue existing and oppressing. This web consists not only of human discrimination, but of non-human forms too; and the human and non-human discriminations both encourage and support each other in the same ways that different types of human discriminations do.

If we always have this hierarchy where animals are at the very bottom, then it’s easy for us to make ourselves unequal by comparing certain groups of people to animals, to the bottom of the hierarchy.
